  • Computational linguists often ascribe to these theories, as the data collection process tends to be more empirical and statistical.

Skill Acquisition Theory:


Skill Acquisition Theory, which describes the neurobiological process of automatization, states that language cognition and comprehension can be learned like any other skill.

Wait, what is automatization? ü§î

Automatization is the solidifying of linguistic knowledge through repeated output. After practicing similar/identical vocabulary or constructions multiple times, our brain is better able to recall accurate forms and their corresponding functions.


This concept can be seen in the Declarative/Procedural model which linguists refer to in acquisition settings. This model is based on data from neurobiology (such as EEG scans to measure electrical brain activity while performing linguistic tasks)

‚Üí Declarative knowledge (comes with practice) ‚Äì instructed use, explicit metalinguistic instruction, better suited for older ages

‚Üí Procedural knowledge (comes with automatization) ‚Äì naturalistic, immersive use, better suited for younger ages



Processability theory:

Dr. Manfred Pienemann proposed his Processability Theory as a subsection of the Interaction Framework.

In this theory, learners are constrained by developmental sequences determined on individual cognitive abilities/prior knowledge. For example, learners need to have a certain capacity in their L2 in order to access their L1 transfer of features, as they at least need to be able to ask ‚Äúhow do I say ___?‚Äù in their L2.


Cognitive-Interactionism:

Cognitive-interactionist thinking borrows from innatism, interactionism, and sociolinguistics.

In this framework, the following requirements are necessary for second language acquisition.

  • Input = lang learners are exposed to examples of output through environment (Krashen)
  • Comprehensible input (i + 1) (aka natural learning)
  • Modified output: Native speakers modify language when talking to a percieved language learner
  • Modified input: The internal pre-modification of a speaker's output ‚Äì most are interactionally-based and at the current level of the learner
  • Interaction, negotiation of meaning
